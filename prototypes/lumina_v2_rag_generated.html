The RAG-Generated Approach

The core paradigm shift

The template-engine approach treats templates as artifacts — versioned, owned, lifecycle-managed. The RAG approach treats templates as training data for an agent that synthesizes documents on demand.

The ~350 historical employment agreements in the corpus stop being 350 things to maintain. They become a sample distribution the agent learns from. Adding a new country means adding a few examples to the corpus, not building a new template.

What this approach removes

  • The template registry. Replaced by a corpus and ingestion pipeline.
  • Template-by-template legal review. Replaced by output sampling and first-time review per combination.
  • Hand-coded eligibility rules. The agent infers what to retrieve from the request context.
  • Template versioning as a primary concern. Replaced by corpus versioning — which texts are canonical at any given moment.

What this approach requires that does not exist today

  • An LLM with strong legal-reasoning behavior, prompted or tuned to maintain legal precision. Current frontier models are close, not perfect.
  • Drift detection. Statistical monitoring of outputs vs. corpus, to flag when the model is generating content unlike anything in the corpus.
  • Audit log of retrievals. For every generated doc, a record of what the model pulled and why — needed for legal defensibility.
  • A defined response to hallucination scenarios. Extractive citations, retrieval-augmented constraints, and sample review form the mitigation layer.

Where humans remain in the loop

  1. Ingestion gate. Corpus updates require legal sign-off before going live.
  2. First-time review. The first generated output for any new (country × event × worker class) combination is human-reviewed.
  3. High-risk classes always reviewed. Senior compensation, cross-border employment, terminations, and other regulated cases.
  4. Drift alerts. Anomaly detection on the output distribution.

Strengths relative to a deterministic template engine

  • Adding the 351st template is effectively free.
  • Updating regulations across N templates simultaneously is one corpus update, not N template edits.
  • Vendor scope for legal/data matrix work becomes annotation on the corpus — faster than rewriting hundreds of templates.
  • The "drop in a new clause for jurisdiction X" workflow is direct: ingest into the corpus, and the next matching generation reflects it.

Weaknesses relative to a deterministic template engine

  • Legal defensibility is harder. "Rendered template v3.2" is auditable; "the LLM said this" is messier.
  • Hallucination risk is real, even with RAG. A wrong clause is potentially a contract dispute.
  • Behavior changes as the model changes. The deterministic engine is reproducible by design; RAG requires versioned model + corpus to be reproducible.
  • Cost per generation is non-zero (LLM tokens). At 16,000 docs per year × N tokens, this is a meaningful operating cost.

Where this approach fits

The most coherent positioning is not "RAG instead of templates" but "RAG where templates can't reach." Deterministic templates are the right fit for high-volume, low-variation, deterministic flows. The RAG agent is the right fit for low-volume, high-variation, novel-jurisdiction flows — a new country opening, a non-standard separation, a custom retention agreement.

The recommended product configuration is: an engine that knows when to call the agent. Default to deterministic templates; route to RAG-generated drafts when the registry has no match or the request crosses pre-set complexity thresholds.
