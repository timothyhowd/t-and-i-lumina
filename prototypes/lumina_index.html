Summary

The dominant framing for document automation has been "speed up the existing template-based workflow." That framing is not wrong, but it under-uses what is now possible with AI, and over-fits to a vendor-led template-harmonization plan.

This document presents four prototypes, each rethinking a different part of the workflow:

  • Approach A — Template Engine. Templates as first-class artifacts with metadata, eligibility rules, and governance. Closest to the current MVP.
  • Approach B — RAG-Generated. Templates become training data. Documents are synthesized at request time from a versioned corpus.
  • Approach C — Regulation-Native. No internal legal language at all. Lawyers attest to rules, not document text. Documents assemble from authoritative sources with citations on every clause.
  • Approach D — Conversational Co-pilot. A specialist describes what they need; the agent gathers context, surfaces edge cases, drafts, and routes to escalation when uncertain.

The recommended path is not any one approach in isolation but a layered stack that combines the conversational surface of D with the engines of A, B, and C running underneath. A working demo of the synthesized stack — labelled simply Lumina — is the recommended primary artifact for stakeholder review.

Lumina — the synthesized stack

A People Ops specialist describes what they need in plain language. Lumina drafts the document using deterministic templates where it can, retrieves precedent where it has to, and cites live regulation everywhere. The specialist reviews and signs off; everything is logged.

Key questions, answered

Could the entire historical template inventory be loaded into an agent that generates the appropriate document at request time?
Yes — that is Approach B. The corpus tab in B shows what an indexed corpus looks like; the generation tab demonstrates the agent retrieving precedent and synthesizing a draft. The "drop in legal update" tab covers the workflow for new legal language being added to the corpus.

Hallucination risk and audit defensibility are real concerns at the volume of ~16,000 documents per year. The recommended Lumina stack mitigates both by wrapping the RAG capability in the conversational specialist-supervised surface from Approach D — same generative capability, with a human signing off on every output.
If a new clause is required for documents in a particular jurisdiction, can it be applied without editing every template?
Yes in B, even better in C. Approach B ingests the new clause into the corpus; the next matching generation reflects it. Approach C goes further: lawyers attest to a single new rule, and that rule applies everywhere automatically — no corpus updates, no template edits, no deployments. The "Regulation Changed Demo" tab in C walks through this end-to-end.
Can authoritative country regulations be tapped directly, with lawyers reviewing rules instead of authoring or maintaining templates?
This is the Approach C thesis. Lawyers stop reviewing documents and start attesting to rules: roughly 200 rules across all jurisdictions vs. 900 templates today. Same legal coverage; far less lawyer time per change.

The constraint: regulatory feed availability is uneven. Some countries publish API-accessible regulatory data (e.g., Spain's BOE). Many do not. Where they do not, the rule database becomes the canonical source — still a meaningful improvement on per-template review, but not free to maintain.
How is content updated with minimal governance overhead?
Each approach answers this differently:
  • A: Edit template → version bump → legal re-review per template. Most overhead.
  • B: Drop new clause into corpus → first ten generations get human review, then sampling. Less overhead; statistical sampling instead of per-doc review.
  • C: Regulator publishes change → rule updated → lawyer attests once → applies everywhere. Minimal overhead, but only for things that map cleanly to "rules."
  • D: Specialist's conversation surfaces relevant new context as it happens; agent references whichever underlying source is most current. Distributed governance, always human-supervised.
The Lumina stack uses A's path for high-volume routine cases, B's path for novel cases, and C's regulation layer to provide the citation framework on outputs from any path.
Where do humans definitely need to remain in the loop?
Six categories, in priority:
  1. First-time generation for any new (event × country × worker class) combination — across all four approaches
  2. High-stakes outputs — senior compensation, performance terminations, cross-border employment
  3. Regulation change verification — before a new rule or clause goes live, a lawyer signs off
  4. Edge cases the system cannot confidently handle — non-EU sponsorship, unusual benefits, novel role types
  5. Sample audit — random spot-checks of generated outputs (5% in B, lower in C once attestations stabilize)
  6. Drift detection alerts — when generated outputs diverge from corpus statistics in unexpected ways
Approach D handles 1, 2, and 4 by design (the human is always in the conversation). B and C require explicit gating workflows for these cases. The Lumina stack inherits D's by-design human-in-the-loop and adds explicit gating from B and C for the additional cases.

Human-in-the-loop policy

Always

  • First-time generation for any new (event × country × worker class) combination
  • Compensation packages above €150K, or any senior leadership offer
  • Cross-border employment (employee country ≠ entity country)
  • Non-EU sponsorship situations
  • Performance terminations past probation
  • Any case where the agent's confidence falls below threshold (escalation as a first-class workflow)

Conditionally

  • Regulation change rollout: first ten outputs after a corpus update or rule change
  • Drift alerts: when generated outputs diverge from corpus statistics
  • Random sampling: 5% of all generated docs, async/weekly

Never

  • Routine variable resolution (employee name, start date, role title)
  • Template selection when only one matches eligibility cleanly
  • Routing to DocuSign vs. AskHR delivery based on signature requirement
  • Storing in Workday under the determined category
  • Notifying signers, sending nudges, logging completion

Suggested sequencing

  1. Phase 1 — Wolt MVP (no change): Ship the upload-automation MVP as planned. Approach A is the right thing for that scope and timeline.
  2. Phase 2 — Conversational surface: Build the Lumina conversation interface using existing templates as the primary backend. This is the demo most likely to land with stakeholders.
  3. Phase 3 — RAG fallback: Pilot Approach B as a fallback engine inside Lumina — invoked only when no template matches. Specialist still in loop; risk low.
  4. Phase 4 — Regulation pilot: Pilot Approach C in one country (e.g., Spain — BOE is API-accessible). Use it to learn what rule attestation means in practice.
  5. Phase 5 — Decision: Decide whether C expands to two or three additional countries based on the pilot, and whether Lumina becomes the default surface or remains specialist-only.

Implication for the vendor RFI

Recommended scope reduction: the four-pillar vendor scope can be cut by approximately three-quarters without losing the work that actually requires external expertise.

  • Keep: Pillar I (legal/data matrix) for the regulation pilot in one country at a time, not all jurisdictions upfront.
  • Cut: Pillar II (modular template development). The Lumina architecture replaces it. Modular legal text creates re-review burden on legal counsel and combinatorial state explosion.
  • Cut: Pillar III (end-to-end design and rollout). This is the platform work to own internally.
  • Cut: Pillar IV (governance and maintenance). This is the operating model to own forever; vendors can advise on initial design but cannot own it.

Net: vendor scope drops materially; internal platform scope grows; total program cost is lower; and the organization owns a real platform instead of inheriting a vendor-built artifact.

Risks acknowledged

If only Approach A ships

The MVP is solved on time. The existing operating model is reinforced. The opportunity to lead on AI-native HR operations is missed. The vendor RFI proceeds, and the wrong stack is inherited.

If Approach B or C is adopted unilaterally

Risk of overcommitting on infrastructure that is not yet proven. Hallucination incidents in B or rule-extraction errors in C could create real legal exposure at scale. Timeline slips become likely.

If Approach D ships alone

It scales linearly with specialists. Rough math: 11 minutes × 16,000 documents = ~2,933 person-hours per year. That's not the productivity unlock the program needs at full scale. Without an automated engine underneath, the labor savings are limited.

The risk of the layered Lumina recommendation

It is harder to explain than a single-paradigm answer. Stakeholders will tend to pull toward one approach. Discipline on what each layer is for is required — without it, the result is three half-built systems instead of one stack with three engines.
