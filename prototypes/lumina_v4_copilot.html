Why this matters for v4

v1 makes the specialist pick the right template from this list. v4 makes the specialist describe what they need; the co-pilot picks. The same 14 templates get used, but the cognitive load shifts from "which file do I open?" to "what am I actually trying to do?" — which is a much shorter conversation.

Crucially: when the specialist describes something the co-pilot can't confidently match (non-EU sponsorship, novel role, ambiguous CBA application), it escalates — exactly what the human-in-the-loop design needs.

The Conversational Co-pilot Approach

The core paradigm shift

The other paradigms — template engine, RAG-generated, regulation-native — aim to remove humans from the document creation loop. The conversational co-pilot aims to make humans dramatically faster while keeping them in the loop, by design.

A specialist describes the situation in natural language. The co-pilot asks structured follow-ups, surfaces context the specialist may not know (regulatory updates, edge-case rules, prior addendums), drafts the document, and allows iterative revision. The specialist signs off — they are never automated out of the workflow.

Why this might be the right primary surface

Loading a large template inventory into an agent is a strong move on the input side. But on the output side, most of the cost in document workflows is not the drafting itself — it is the judgment surrounding it. Should this person be permanent or fixed-term? Is the role still under the CBA threshold? Does this change trigger pension implications? A specialist asks themselves these questions every time. The co-pilot makes those questions explicit, surfaces what the agent knows about each, and lets the specialist decide.

What this approach removes

  • The "specialist opens a Word file" workflow. Replaced with conversation.
  • The cognitive load of selecting the right template from a long inventory. Replaced with description-driven retrieval.
  • The class of "I forgot to update the CBA reference" errors. The co-pilot enforces these as it drafts.
  • The bright line between "automated" and "manual." Replaced with a spectrum: trivial cases breeze through; complex cases get more conversation; pathological cases escalate.

What this approach requires that does not exist today

  • An LLM tuned for HR domain, or a strong general LLM with rich corpus and careful prompting. Achievable, but real engineering work.
  • Conversation state management. The co-pilot must remember context across turns and across specialists working on the same employee.
  • Confidence calibration. The agent must know when it does not know — and escalate cleanly. This is the hardest part of the system.
  • Tight integration with HR systems. When a specialist names an employee, the co-pilot must look the record up in Workday, see the current contract, and reason from there.

Where humans remain in the loop

  1. Always. The specialist owns the conversation. The agent cannot finalize a document alone.
  2. Surface, don't decide. When the agent knows a relevant rule (pension threshold, CBA limit, sponsorship requirement), it surfaces it as guidance — the specialist confirms or overrides.
  3. Escalation is first-class. Cases the agent cannot confidently complete route to Counsel as part of the workflow, not as an afterthought.
  4. Audit trail is the conversation. Every piece of context surfaced and every choice made is logged.

Strengths

  • Incremental. Specialists keep using the same templates, just through a different interface. No big-bang migration.
  • Absorbs other paradigms. The co-pilot can use a RAG corpus for retrieval and pull from regulation feeds for grounding — but the human-facing surface is the conversation.
  • High audit defensibility. "A trained HR specialist made every decision, with AI support" is a substantially easier story to tell Legal and audit than "the AI generated this autonomously."
  • Demonstrably AI-native without overpromising. A working chat-driven document drafter looks like a real AI product — without claiming the AI is doing more than can be defensibly justified.

Weaknesses

  • It does not fully eliminate human time. Approximately 11 minutes per document × 16,000 docs = ~2,933 person-hours per year. That is still significant.
  • It depends on specialist quality. If specialists rubber-stamp the agent's draft, the result is the RAG-approach risks without the productivity unlock.
  • It scales linearly with people. The other paradigms scale with infrastructure.

Where this approach fits

The most coherent positioning is as the primary surface for a layered stack — the conversational interface a specialist actually uses, sitting on top of deterministic templates for high-volume cases, a RAG agent for novel cases, and a regulation layer for citations. Different cases route to different engines under the hood; the specialist sees one consistent conversation.

Product sentence: "A People Ops specialist describes what they need; the system drafts it using deterministic templates where it can, generative synthesis where it has to, and live regulatory citations everywhere; the specialist reviews and signs off; everything is logged."

That's not three or four competing approaches. It's one product with multiple engines under it and a conversational human interface on top.
