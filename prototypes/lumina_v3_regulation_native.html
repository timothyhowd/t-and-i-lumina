The Regulation-Native Approach

The core paradigm shift

The template-engine approach owns templates. The RAG approach owns a corpus. The regulation-native approach owns nothing. Legal language lives at its source of authority — statute, regulator, collective bargaining agreement. Those sources are queried at generation time. Lawyers attest to rules, not document text.

What this approach removes

  • The template registry. Gone.
  • The corpus. Gone — no internally stored legal text.
  • Per-template legal review. Replaced by per-rule attestation: ~200 rules across all jurisdictions vs. ~900 templates.
  • "What version of the law applies?" ambiguity. Always: whatever the source said at the moment of generation. Logged. Citable.

What this approach requires that does not exist today

  • Authoritative regulation feeds. Some countries publish API-accessible regulatory data. Many do not. Where feeds are unavailable, internal counsel must maintain a structured rule database — which becomes its own canonical source.
  • Rule extraction infrastructure. Turning regulatory prose into structured rules ("notice period: 15 days during probation") is non-trivial. Can be human-curated or LLM-assisted.
  • Citation and provenance system. Every clause in every document must trace back to a specific rule attestation, which traces back to a specific regulation version.
  • Stale-source detection. If a regulatory feed goes stale for 48 hours, generation in that jurisdiction must pause. Explicit policy required.

Where humans remain in the loop

  1. Rule attestation. Lawyers sign off on each rule before it goes active. Re-sign on regulation change.
  2. Source onboarding. Vetting that a regulatory feed is canonical for a jurisdiction.
  3. Cross-rule conflicts. When statute and collective bargaining agreement conflict, a human decides which prevails.
  4. Output spot-checks. Sampled outputs to confirm rule-to-text rendering is correct. Low frequency, since rules are deterministic once attested.

Strengths

  • Always current. No "did we update template X for the new law?" — the law itself is the source.
  • Defensible. Every clause cites a regulation. Suitable for audit.
  • Scales across countries efficiently. Adding a new country requires wiring up the regulatory feed and attesting its rules. ~200 rules vs. ~900 templates.
  • Regulation changes propagate automatically: a new requirement in jurisdiction X reflects in the next generation; only the new rule needs attestation.

Weaknesses

  • Source availability is uneven. Some jurisdictions have machine-readable regulatory feeds; many do not.
  • Regulations don't capture everything. Company-specific terms (equity grants, sign-on bonuses, custom benefits) aren't in any regulator's database. A hybrid layer is required.
  • Rule extraction is its own hard problem. Real regulations are messy. Turning them into clean rules is months of legal-technical work per country.
  • Failure mode is severe. If the rule database is wrong, every document generated is wrong simultaneously.

Where this approach fits

The regulation-native approach is the most future-proof of the four paradigms but also the most expensive to build. The most coherent positioning is as a layered citation system rather than a standalone replacement for templates or RAG. Regulatory updates flow through the rule layer regardless of which engine actually generates the document; templates and RAG provide the body, the rule layer provides the citations.

A reasonable phased adoption: pilot in one jurisdiction with a strong machine-readable regulatory feed, validate what rule attestation means in practice, and expand based on what is learned.
