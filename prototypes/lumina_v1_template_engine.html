Why the abstraction matters

Nothing in the engine is template-specific. The same code paths handle a Finnish offer letter, a US separation agreement, and a contingent worker addendum. The work to add the 11th, 100th, or 900th template is configuration, not engineering — provided each new template conforms to the schema. That is the lever.

A vendor scope premised on harmonizing 900 templates as a precondition is solving the wrong problem. What is needed is governance that enforces schema conformance as templates onboard, incrementally.

Position paper · Internal

The Template Engine Approach

Approach A · template-engine paradigm · standalone analysis

Summary

  1. A template-as-platform-capability approach is the right architectural starting point for high-volume, deterministic document workflows.
  2. The narrow upload-automation MVP is empirically validating the platform-capability thesis at small scale: ~16,000 documents handled by a thin abstraction layer, no template harmonization required up front.
  3. The right next step is to generalize the MVP into an abstract engine + governance framework, then onboard templates incrementally as they pass quality gates — rather than commission a multi-pillar vendor scope to harmonize the entire template inventory in advance.
  4. Vendor scope premised on full template harmonization should be compressed substantially: retain support for legal/data matrix work in regulated countries; cut the modular template development, central library construction, and governance ownership pillars.

The strategic tension

The prevailing T&I capability strategy explicitly excludes:

"Out of scope: Retroactively migrate historical documents at scale… Enforce deep template governance through technology… Own or govern document content, legal language, or policy decisions."

By contrast, a typical RFI scope asks the vendor to:

  • Audit ~900 templates and consolidate them (retroactive harmonization at scale)
  • Build a master legal/data matrix for every operating country (deep governance through tooling)
  • Design modular "Lego-block" templates with country add-ons (template-shape governance)
  • Establish a central library with strict version control (governance through technology)
These two scopes are incompatible. Running both produces conflicting deliverables, conflicting ownership, and a vendor-built platform inheriting in violation of the published strategy.

What the prototype demonstrates

The interactive prototype (Generate Document tab) shows that a single abstract engine — no per-template code — can route a hire event for a Finnish engineer and a separation event for a US contingent worker through identical machinery. The work to add the 11th template, the 100th, or the 900th is configuration, not engineering, provided each template conforms to the schema.

The lever is not template count. The lever is whether governance enforces schema conformance as the price of admission. That is a People Ops + Legal + T&I joint operating model — not a vendor deliverable.

Three honest constraints

1. Some assumptions about workflow generalization have already been invalidated

Adding contingent workers to the upload-automation scope added engineering effort that the original "annual cycle generalizes" assumption did not predict. That is a small but real signal: template variance has a non-zero engineering cost. The implication is not that vendor template harmonization is correct — it is that the abstraction must be designed to accommodate variance through metadata, not avoid variance through standardization.

2. Filename-keyword dispatch is brittle and will not carry the long-term solution

The MVP works because filename keywords happen to map cleanly to Workday categories. Once additional brands or evolving categories are introduced, keyword dispatch fragments. The MVP is a stepping stone, not a destination. The platform investment required is a proper variable + metadata schema and a registry — which is what this prototype demonstrates.

3. The strategy's caution about "deep template governance through technology" needs refinement

This prototype enforces structural correctness through technology — variable schema, eligibility rules, conformance gates. That is a form of governance through technology. The defensible distinction is: structural governance through technology, content governance by humans. Schema, variables, metadata, and signature flow are appropriate to enforce in software. Legal language, regional nuance, and policy remain human-owned.

Why "modular Lego-block templates" is an anti-pattern

A vendor scope proposing modular templates with plug-and-play country add-ons creates several problems:

  • Modular legal text means every assembly of clauses requires re-review. Legal review cycles get worse, not better.
  • Country add-ons assume country variation is the dominant axis. It is not — worker type, role band, compensation plan, and entity also vary, and these axes intersect combinatorially.
  • The right architecture is the inverse: treat templates as monolithic legal artifacts, layer metadata on top. Variation lives in metadata and variable resolution, not in template body assembly.

Recommended path

  1. Continue the upload-automation MVP as planned. Do not disrupt downstream dependencies (e.g., annual cycle).
  2. Generalize the MVP architecture into the abstract engine as the next major increment. Replace filename-keyword dispatch with the proper schema. Treat this as the platform foundation.
  3. Stand up the governance operating model in parallel — Template Owner role, intake rubric, conformance gates, RACI. People Ops + Legal + T&I joint commitment, no vendor required.
  4. Compress vendor scope:
    • Keep: legal/data matrix work, selectively for regulated markets only
    • Cut: modular template development and central library construction — replaced by registry + governance
    • Cut: end-to-end design and rollout — this is internal platform work
    • Cut: governance and maintenance ownership — this is an operating model owned permanently in-house
  5. Onboard templates incrementally — set a pace (e.g., 20 templates per month) with a hard quality gate. Stop the line if conformance drops.

Cost implication

A 12–18 month vendor engagement covering the full four-pillar scope at consultancy rates is typically a seven-figure commitment. Compressing to selective legal/data matrix work likely reduces that by approximately 70–80%. The larger saving is avoided permanence: vendor-built modular template systems become accidental infrastructure that requires ongoing in-house maintenance.

If the abstraction is built correctly, the next several hundred templates onboard at the speed of governance, not the speed of engineering. That is the structural unlock.

Operating Model & Governance Framework

The framework that makes the prototype real. Without this, the engine is a demo. With this, it's a durable capability.

First principles

  1. Structure is governed; content is owned. The engine enforces variable schema, eligibility rules, metadata mapping. Humans (Legal, People Ops) own legal language and policy.
  2. Governance is operational, not architectural. Templates onboard incrementally through quality gates — not through a one-time vendor harmonization project.
  3. Schema is the contract. Templates that don't conform don't run. No exceptions, no workarounds.
  4. The system of record is canonical. Workday is the source of truth for storage; observability lives outside Workday so failures are diagnosable.
  5. Vendors are capacity multipliers, not owners. Time-boxed, deliverable-bound, with explicit handoff criteria.

Vendor exit criteria (if engaged)

  • All Pillar I deliverables (legal/data matrix) checked into the registry under People Ops ownership
  • Country variation rules documented and translated into engine eligibility DSL by T&I
  • No vendor-built modular template assemblies remain in production
  • Knowledge transfer sessions delivered to People Ops Template Owners (recorded)
  • 30-day handoff period during which People Ops operates without vendor support before sign-off
  • No ongoing maintenance retainer; bug fixes capped at 30 days post-acceptance

Success measures (re-using the strategy doc)

  • Template onboarding velocity — templates moving from intake to Active per month, with conformance breach rate stable
  • First-attempt upload success — % of completed documents stored to correct profile + category on first try (target ≥99%)
  • Mean time to diagnosis — time from failed routing to actionable error report (target < 10 min)
  • Conformance health — % of Active templates with green status across all gates (target ≥95%)
  • Legal review currency — % of Active templates within 12-month review window (target 100%)
  • Engine code change rate — engineering changes per new template (target = 0)

The bet

If this operating model works, by Q4 2026 you should be able to onboard a new template in under 10 business days, end-to-end, with no engineering changes — driven entirely by governance throughput.

If it doesn't, the conformance metrics will tell you within 60 days. You'll have lost a quarter, not a year and seven figures.
